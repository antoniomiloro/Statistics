Th10: The Wiener process and the GBM, Derivations and simulations

The Wiener process, also known as Brownian motion, is a continuous-time stochastic process where the change in the variable is proportional to the square root of the time interval and a random Gaussian variable¹.
It is often used to model random events that happen over time¹.
A Wiener process is a real-valued continuous-time stochastic process named in honor of American mathematician Norbert Wiener¹.
It is often also called Brownian motion due to its historical connection with the physical process of the same name originally observed by Scottish botanist Robert Brown¹.

Properties:

- The Wiener process is characterized by the following properties¹:
  - W0 = 0 almost surely.
  - W has independent increments: for every t > 0, the future increments Wt+u - Wt, u ≥ 0, are independent of the past values Ws, s < t.
  - W has Gaussian increments: Wt+u - Wt is normally distributed with mean 0 and variance u, Wt+u - Wt ∼ N(0,u).

The Wiener process can be derived from the fundamental solution of the heat equation, which is a partial differential equation that describes the diffusion of heat in a medium.
The solution is given by the formula:
\[u(x,t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{4 \pi t}} e^{-\frac{x^2}{4t}}\]

This function represents the probability density of finding a particle at position x at time t, given that it started at the origin at time 0.
If we let X_t be the random variable that denotes the position of the particle at time t, then we have:
\[X_t \sim N(0, 2t)\]

To obtain the Wiener process, we simply take the difference of two independent normal variables:
\[W_t = X_t - X_0 \sim N(0, 2t) - N(0, 0) = N(0, 2t)\]
