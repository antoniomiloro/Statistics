Th3: The Gaussian Distribution Meaning, Derivations, Simulations

The Gaussian distribution, also known as the **normal distribution**, is a probability distribution that is widely used for modeling continuous random variables ¹³.
It is a bell-shaped curve that is dependent on the mean (μ) and the standard deviation (σ) ¹. The probability density function of the univariate Gaussian distribution is given by:
$$f(x, \mu, \sigma) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}$$
where:
  - f(x) is the probability density of obtaining the value xx.
  - μ is the mean of the distribution.
  - σ is the standard deviation of the distribution.
  - π is the value of pi (approximately 3.14159).
The peak of the graph is always located at the mean and the area under the curve is always exactly equal to 1 ¹. 68% of all the values lie within one standard deviation of the mean.
At 2σ, this increases to 95%, and 99.7% of the values lie within 3σ of the mean ¹.
The demonstration requires calculating the integral of the pdf over the entire actual range, which guarantees that the total area under the curve is equal to 1 (as it represents the total probability).
However, this process involves highly advanced integral calculus and mathematical analysis techniques that are intricate and require extensive calculus and probability knowledge.
For a comprehensive presentation of the normal distribution, it is recommended that one refers to advanced statistical or probability textbooks as a full demonstration would necessitate significant space and detailed mathematical notation.
Here is a simulation of the Gaussian distribution with mean 0 and standard deviation 1:
import numpy as np import matplotlib.pyplot as plt mu, sigma = 0, 1 # mean and standard deviation s = np.random.normal(mu, sigma, 1000) count, bins, ignored = plt.hist(s, 30, density=True) plt.plot(bins, 1/(sigma * np.sqrt(2 * np.pi)) * np.exp( - (bins - mu)**2 / (2 * sigma**2) ), linewidth=2, color='r') plt.show()
