Th12: Ito Integration and Calculus, Concept and Didactical Simulations

Itô calculus, named after Kiyosi Itô, extends the methods of calculus to stochastic processes such as Brownian motion.
The central concept is the Itô stochastic integral, a stochastic generalization of the Riemann–Stieltjes integral in analysis. The integrands and the integrators are now stochastic processes.
The Itô integral can be defined as long as the integrand H is adapted, which loosely speaking means that its value at time t can only depend on information available up until this time.
Roughly speaking, one chooses a sequence of partitions of the interval from 0 to t and constructs Riemann sums. The limit then is taken in probability as the mesh of the partition is going to zero.

Itô’s formula has the form:
\[f(X_t) = f(X_0) + \int_0^t f'(X_s) dX_s + \frac{1}{2} \int_0^t f''(X_s) d[X]_s\]

where f is a twice-differentiable function, is a stochastic variable, f’ and f’’ are its first and second derivatives, and is the quadratic variation of , which measures how much varies over time.
The quadratic variation term is what makes Itô’s formula different from the usual chain rule, and it accounts for the randomness of the stochastic variable.
In mathematical finance, the described evaluation strategy of the integral is conceptualized as that we are first deciding what to do, then observing the change in the prices.
The integrand is how much stock we hold, the integrator represents the movement of the prices, and the integral is how much money we have in total including what our stock is worth, at any given moment.
