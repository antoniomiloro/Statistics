Th5: Statistical Distributions: Continuous, Discrete, Properties and simulations

Statistical distributions are mathematical functions that describe the likelihood of different outcomes from a random process. They are divided into two main types: discrete and continuous distributions²⁴⁵.

- Discrete distributions:
Discrete distributions are used when the outcomes are countable, like the number of heads when flipping a coin. It is a variable that has a value with finite (integer) interval and has a finite limit.
Examples include the binomial and Poisson distributions².
  - Binomial Distribution: Suppose you flip a coin 10 times. The number of times it lands on heads follows a binomial distribution. Each flip is an independent event, and the probability of success (landing on heads) is the same for each flip¹.
  - Poisson Distribution>: If you're counting the number of emails you receive in a day, this could follow a Poisson distribution. The Poisson distribution models the number of events happening in a fixed interval of time or space³.

- Continuous distributions:
Continuous distributions, on the other hand, are used when the outcomes can take on any value within a certain range, like the measurement of height or weight. These outcomes can have infinite decimal places.
Examples include the normal and exponential distributions⁴⁵.
  - Normal Distribution: Human heights follow a normal distribution. Most people are of average height, while fewer people are very short or very tall¹.
  - Exponential Distribution: If you're waiting for a bus and the time between buses is exponentially distributed, then the time you have to wait for the next bus follows an exponential distribution⁴.

Each distribution has its own properties, such as the mean (average), variance (spread), skewness (asymmetry), and kurtosis (tailedness).
These properties help describe the shape and characteristics of the distribution¹.

- Simulations:
Simulations often use these distributions to generate random variables that mimic real-world randomness. For instance, a simulation of a call center might use a Poisson distribution to model the number of incoming calls per hour³.
Remember, the choice of distribution depends on the nature of the process and the type of data you are dealing with².
